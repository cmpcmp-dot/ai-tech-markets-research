## The AI Tech Stack

**Ganesh Sitaraman [5:35]:** It's a great question, and I'm going to come at it a little bit backwards, but I'll come back to it in a second. Because I think one way to engage this question is — just let me first ask folks around here: how many people have used ChatGPT in some form? Tried it out? Tested it? Almost everybody. Whose hands are not up? So for the people whose hands are not up — I hope you've heard of AI? Maybe? Okay.

So if you've used it, you're engaging with an application, right? It's something consumer-facing, you can use. So ChatGPT is your application. But how does it work? It's actually running on a model, which OpenAI runs. There's a big foundation model underneath that, that was trained on all this data. And that data had to come from somewhere, and had to be collected, and it had to be worked through by the model. And to do all that training, to create the model that could actually work — that takes a lot of computer processing power.

And all that processing power happens from cloud computing, which is another set of businesses that run all these big server farms. It says "cloud," but you know, these are places on land — they're not up in the sky. And so the cloud computing, the processing power there, all comes from chips — semiconductors. And as you move down that funnel from application to models, down to cloud, down to chips, it turns out there's fewer and fewer players at those levels.

You can imagine having a lot of application developers, but it's much harder to be a foundation model developer, because you have to get all this data and you also have to have the money to be able to train the model on the cloud. And to produce the cloud, you have to buy these chips — and the chips are only made by one company, at the most, for high-end technical chips that are the fastest. And they're extremely expensive, and so most companies can't afford that. And so we have this shrinking level of competition.

So to get to your question — I think the way I think about the question is: where should we have competition, and where should we not expect competition to be possible? If you think about the cloud sector, for example — there are very few companies that can provide the scale in that. And right now there's three big ones: Amazon, Google, and Microsoft. If you go even further down to chip manufacturing — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Corporation is a major, the major player there, the dominant player. If you go to the machines that make the chips that they use — there's only one company in the whole world, a Dutch company called ASML, that makes that equipment.

So at these levels, and there's so much expense, there's so much know-how needed that I think it's unlikely we're going to see 200 efficient, very large cloud companies operating. And so the analogy I think of at these lower layers in what's called the AI tech stack — in cloud and models — is it may be more like an electric company. You actually don't want 20 electric wires coming to your house — it would be extremely messy and inconvenient in your front yard or backyard. You want one. But the problem is, if you have one, you have a monopoly, and they'll probably give you higher prices and worse service.

And so I think the way I think about it is: we might want a kind of regulated system that recognizes we're probably going to have an oligopoly in some of these markets, but we need those regulations in place to ensure that at the higher levels of the stack — applications and so on — we actually can have developers come in, we can have startups, we can have a very robust competitive environment there. And that, I think, is how we do things in a lot of other places. And that's really the history of this utility stuff — we have the regulated electricity company, but anybody can start a company that uses electricity and relies on it. And I think that's a way to think about the problem.

## Applying Antimonopoly Tools to AI

**Chris Hughes [9:31]:** You have this really great diagram. For those of you who haven't seen Ganesh's reports, you really have to go look at them. There's a summary that is digestible, eight or nine pages, and then there's the big 100-plus-page thing for the experts. But there's this diagram that's really helped me, at least, where you see the foundation being the chip manufacturers. On top of that, you see the cloud and the already-existing oligopolistic structure there. On top of that, you see the models themselves that OpenAI and other folks are building. And then on top of that, finally, you see the apps that are interacting with it.

So your analysis effectively — at least how I read it — suggests that we should think about each one of those levels separately. So when people ask, "Well, what are you going to do about AI?" — that's not really a sensical question, because we need to talk about: what are we going to do about the chip manufacturers? What are we going to do about the cloud? What are we going to do about the models? What are we going to do about the apps? And how each one of those layers presents different kind of problems — at least as I was experiencing your research.

**Ganesh Sitaraman [10:32]:** Yeah, I think that's exactly right. And that's a framework that I think, for most people — I certainly found it was helpful for me — could be a way to understand where the conversation is and can be. So if we're thinking about antimonopoly tools like breakups — I think one of the things we've talked about before — I think you got this at some points on the Facebook questions: what does it even mean to break up Facebook? You want to have 20 — is there going to be a New England Facebook, Southern Facebook? Right? I mean, there are questions like that that people would come up with. But it actually means thinking separately, vertically, about these different parts in the stack, as opposed to thinking we're going to break up each one of these.

**Chris Hughes [11:14]:** Well, I do — I want to spend one minute on this because you're a lawyer, I'm sure there are many lawyers in the room, but we're not here for a legal seminar this evening. So just at a high level — if people say, "Well, what does antimonopoly and AI even mean? We're not going to break up OpenAI." In your report, you talk about structural separations, nondiscrimination, licensing — just give us a flavor of the menu. We don't have time to go through each one of these, but what are some of the tools that we should be thinking about when we think about applying antimonopoly to these AI companies?

**Ganesh Sitaraman [11:52]:** Yeah, it's a great question. So let me start with some of the kind of traditional tools of network, platform, utility regulation — things that have existed for hundreds of years, in railroads and airlines and energy and all these different sectors — that I think are very portable to the tech context.

So one is **structural separations**. The basic idea here is that if you run a kind of platform business, you shouldn't also be able to run the things that are dependent on the platform to exist. And an easy example — that legal speak for breakup, it's a breakup — but it's not a breakup like we're going to make Coke into two companies that make sodas. It's a breakup vertically.

The example I'll give you is the old one: Teddy Roosevelt's administration passes the Hepburn Act, 1906, and it says: railroads — great that you want to run a railroad, but if you're going to transport coal, you can't have a coal company also. Because what's going to happen is you're going to only transport your coal, and you're not going to transport anybody else's coal. And coal companies can't build a railroad every time they want to get their coal somewhere. And so they said, we're going to separate that out. That's a breakup of the railroad that also owns the coal company.

And so here, I think there's a question: are you, as a cloud provider — do you also own a foundation model? Answer: many of the companies either are heavily invested in them or have their own model. Do you also own applications? Yes, they do, in a lot of cases, as well. And so you have these conglomerates that are set up vertically, where you could leverage your power from one sector into the other. And the challenge is: what's to stop you, for example, from copying the idea of a new startup and saying, "You know what, why don't we take that application idea, we'll just build it into our product or our system, and then no one will use that one and it'll just be ours"? And one real downside to that is: if you're going to run a startup — why would you ever start a company then? Because you know your idea is just going to get taken, and you won't make any money out of it.

So we got structural separation. **Non-discrimination** — nondiscrimination rules are similar. The idea here, though, is instead of separating, or after you've separated, you still have to have a rule that says: railroad, you got to take the coal from all the companies. You can't just pick and choose which ones you want to be the winners and losers. So we say you have to take all comers. You got to serve everyone at the same price. It's got to be a transparent price — can't have secret deals. So if I'm Microsoft owning the cloud, I can't just privilege OpenAI because I like them. I would have to allow any model to use the cloud. Take other people on an even playing field. Everyone has to have a level playing field on it.

Another antimonopoly tool that comes up a lot — it's a little bit different, but I want to make sure we talk about it — is a **public option**. The idea here is you have a publicly provided service that coexists with the private option, and that provides a measure of competitive discipline, because you could choose to exit the private option and use the public one instead. But it also ensures access for other uses that might not necessarily be the ones that the private actors can facilitate or prefer to facilitate.

And so we have these all over the place — public schools, there's also private schools. There's public libraries and private ones. Public parks and private ones, and so on and so forth. And so here we could think about a public cloud system, for example — a public option for cloud, where you'd have a public supercomputer, for example, which has all these resources, could be used for scientists and researchers. And that would be something that would coexist with these private clouds. And that's an antimonopoly tool as well.

## Institutions and Implementation

**Chris Hughes [15:27]:** That makes sense. All right, I have one more question, then we're going to you guys for two. Can you talk a little bit about whether or not the existing institutions that we have — specifically, well, there are many institutions, but I'm thinking in particular of the FTC right now — are sufficient for implementing an antimonopoly agenda? Or do we need not only new legislation but new institutions, given the challenges that are ahead of it?

**Ganesh Sitaraman [15:55]:** I think we could do either, but I think it would be better to have new legislation and some new institutions. And the reason why is: the Federal Trade Commission, I think, does great work, and I think they need to be doing work in this space, because of how important it is, and because we don't have action from Congress. But the Federal Trade Commission is also responsible for a lot of other areas of law, and historically brings a lot of cases. They do have rulemaking authority — it gets used a little bit less — but it would be nice to have legislation that affirmatively provided rules that were passed by statute, like nondiscrimination rules and structural separations, gave an agency authority to implement them and flesh out everything.

And part of the reason I think this is important is: the Supreme Court has increasingly been — let's say — less deferential to executive branch agencies. And in a world where the court may strike down regulations of different types, some clarity from Congress would be helpful in insulating that from potential judicial review.
