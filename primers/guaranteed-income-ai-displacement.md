# Guaranteed income as a response to AI displacement: a policy primer

*Prepared for policy staff and advocates. Last updated 2026-07-09.*

## Purpose

This primer answers a narrow question: does the evidence support guaranteed income and related cash-transfer policies as a tool for helping workers through AI-driven labor market disruption, and what would a credible policy design look like. It draws on this session's adversarially fact-checked research (each claim below carries its verification status) plus the curated evidence base already maintained in this project's research tracker (`data/tracker-data.js`, `POLICY_DATA` entries `guaranteed-income`, `ubi`, `ai-dividend`, `sovereign-wealth-fund`).

A sourcing note up front: claims marked **[verified]** passed a 3-vote adversarial fact-check against primary sources this week. Claims marked **[tracker]** come from this project's existing curated database, itself fact-checked in a prior review pass (2026-06-26) but not independently re-verified in this session. Several widely circulated statistics were tested and **failed** verification this round; they are flagged explicitly in the appendix so they don't reappear in a future draft.

---

## 1. The bottom line

AI-driven labor disruption is real, but right now it looks like a narrow, entry-level phenomenon, not a broad unemployment shock. Guaranteed income pilots, meanwhile, show a consistent pattern: recipients use unconditional cash to stabilize (rent, food, transportation, debt), and in the best-designed U.S. trials, cash did not reduce full-time employment and in one case coincided with a *larger* full-time employment gain than the control group. That combination makes cash transfers a credible, evidence-backed transition-support tool today, well before any hypothetical mass-displacement scenario materializes. The caveat is proportion: every pilot behind this evidence is small (hundreds to low thousands of participants, 2-3 years), and the funding-mechanism and legislative landscape is far less settled than the labor-market and pilot-outcome evidence. The primer's job is to keep those two confidence levels distinct.

---

## 2. What we know about AI-driven displacement

**The effect so far is concentrated in early-career workers in the most AI-exposed occupations, not a broad unemployment rise.** Stanford's Digital Economy Lab, using ADP payroll microdata, finds a 13-16% relative employment decline for workers aged 22-25 in the most AI-exposed occupations since generative AI's widespread adoption, even after controlling for firm-level shocks — while experienced workers in the same occupations, and all workers in low-exposure occupations, have held stable or grown. **[verified, high confidence]**

**Anthropic's own labor-market research corroborates this from a different data source.** It finds no statistically significant rise in overall unemployment for AI-exposed workers since ChatGPT's release, but does find that entry-level hiring into highly AI-exposed occupations has slowed by roughly half a percentage point per month. The two findings converge on the same picture: displacement is showing up as a hiring and entry effect on young workers, not as layoffs or a broad unemployment shock. **[verified, high confidence; note the hiring-rate figure is, per the authors' own framing, "just barely statistically significant"]**

**Mainstream macro forecasts do not support a mass-displacement narrative.** Goldman Sachs Research projects generative AI will raise developed-market labor productivity by roughly 15% once fully adopted, pushing unemployment up by about half a percentage point above trend during the transition — and explicitly rejects large-scale, persistent structural unemployment over the next decade, citing that 60% of current U.S. workers hold jobs that did not exist in 1940. **[verified, high confidence]** A more specific, frequently cited Goldman "6-7% of jobs displaced" figure did **not** survive this round's verification and should not be cited. **[refuted, 1-2 vote]**

**What this means for the primer's framing:** the case for guaranteed income as an AI-displacement response should be built as *precautionary and targeted-first*, not as a response to a mass-unemployment event that hasn't happened. The strongest current argument is that entry-level and early-career workers in exposed occupations are the population showing measurable harm today, which argues for targeted transition support (wage insurance, targeted guaranteed income, sectoral retraining) ahead of a universal, economy-wide cash floor.

## 3. What guaranteed income pilots tell us about cash as transition support

**SEED Stockton** ($500/month unconditional, 24 months, Feb 2019-Jan 2021, ~125-131 treatment recipients vs. ~200 control, targeting below-median-income census tracts, funded in part by a $1M grant from the Economic Security Project) found no negative employment impacts. Recipients gained full-time employment at more than twice the rate of the control group (28% to 40%, versus 32% to 37% for controls), and reported reduced income volatility (46.4% versus 67.5% monthly income fluctuation) both before and during the pandemic. **[verified, high confidence, multiple independent corroborating sources]**

**The OpenResearch/Sam Altman-funded Unconditional Income Study (ORUS)** — the largest U.S. cash RCT to date (1,000 low-to-moderate-income adults aged 21-40 receiving $1,000/month vs. 2,000 controls receiving $50/month, Nov 2020-Oct 2023) — found the $1,000/month transfer increased measured household spending by at least $310/month, concentrated in food, rent, and transportation. Recipients used the money predominantly to stabilize basic needs. **[verified, high confidence]** The same study also found a real, non-trivial labor-supply effect worth stating plainly for a balanced primer: recipients' labor force participation fell by roughly 4 percentage points, and work hours declined by 1-2 hours per week on average, relative to controls, with no clearly offsetting increase in job search intensity or job quality. This finding comes from the same rigorous RCT and should be read alongside the spending-stabilization result, not omitted from it. **(This specific labor-supply figure was extracted from the primary NBER working paper w32719 in this research pass but was not one of the claims put through this round's adversarial verification; treat it as well-established in the existing literature rather than freshly re-confirmed here, and re-verify before using it in an external-facing document.)**

**The Compton Pledge** shows that program design, not just transfer size, shapes outcomes: recipients who received payments twice-monthly (versus the same total amount quarterly) showed a significant reduction in credit card debt, while quarterly recipients did not. **[verified, high confidence]** A more specific claim about Compton's exact household counts (695 treatment / 1,402 control) and a "no effect on labor supply for full-time workers" framing did **not** survive verification this round because of an internal duration inconsistency in the source and should not be cited with those specific figures pending a cleaner source check. **[refuted, 0-3 vote]**

**The Jain Family Institute's December 2024 synthesis report** pulls these pilot findings together and positions itself as a fair-minded arbiter between GI critics (who read mixed pilot results as evidence of minimal benefit) and GI supporters (who read the same results as validation). Worth flagging for readers: JFI discloses an institutionally pro-GI starting position even while attempting to represent critics fairly. **[verified, medium confidence on the "neutral arbiter" framing]**

**Denver Basic Income Project, GiveDirectly's Kenya study, and the Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend** are all real, well-documented programs already cited elsewhere in this project's tracker (`{{cite:146}}` for GiveDirectly's AI-supported cash-transfer targeting work; the Alaska PFD is described directly in the `ubi` policy card as distributing $1,312 per resident in 2023). This session's fresh adversarial check tested one specific Denver claim (its October 2022 program-start date framing) and it did not survive verification, likely a sourcing precision issue rather than evidence the program doesn't exist. **Before citing specific dollar figures or outcome statistics from these three programs in an external-facing document, re-run them through a fresh verification pass** — they are directionally reliable per the tracker's existing curated base but were not re-confirmed in this session.

**What this means for the primer's framing:** the honest version of this evidence base is "cash transfers reliably stabilize recipients and do not collapse full-time employment; in the best-known RCT, they modestly reduce total labor supply at the margin." Both halves of that sentence are true and both should appear together. Advocates weaken their own case by citing only the employment-gain result from Stockton without acknowledging the modest labor-supply reduction found in the larger, longer OpenResearch trial — the two studies differ in program design, sample, and duration, and a serious primer says so rather than picking the more convenient number.

## 4. Policy design options

### Universal versus targeted

The tracker's existing `guaranteed-income` (targeted, $800-1,200/month, phased out for higher earners, IRS-administered) and `ubi` (universal, $1,200-2,000/month, no phase-out) policy entries lay out the core tradeoff: universal transfers eliminate means-testing administration and benefit-cliff distortions but cost far more at scale and direct dollars to households that don't need them; targeted transfers concentrate resources on displaced or at-risk workers but require income verification, which reintroduces administrative burden and stigma. **[tracker]**

Given Section 2's finding that measurable AI displacement is currently concentrated among early-career workers in specific exposed occupations, a **targeted-first, expandable design** is the better-evidenced starting point: it matches the population showing actual harm today, it can be piloted and evaluated before a universal commitment, and it builds the administrative infrastructure (IRS-based disbursement, on the advance Child Tax Credit model) that a future universal system would also need. **[tracker + analysis]**

### Funding mechanisms

Four mechanisms recur in the literature and in the tracker's `tax-wealth` category:

- **The Windfall Clause** (GovAI/O'Keefe et al., 2020) proposes that AI firms make an ex ante commitment to donate a significant share of any future extremely large ("windfall") profits from transformative AI to public benefit. This remains a purely theoretical policy design — no firm has adopted it. **[verified, high confidence: it is unproven and untested, not an operating policy]**
- **AI productivity dividend**, modeled on the Alaska Permanent Fund, would distribute a share of AI-attributable productivity growth as a periodic payment, financed through a corporate tax surcharge on AI-intensive sectors or a levy on AI patent licensing revenue. **[tracker]** The administrative challenge — isolating AI-attributable productivity growth from other capital investment — is unresolved, and no country has implemented an AI-specific version.
- **Sovereign wealth fund** accumulation, paired with a dividend, has drawn unusual bipartisan interest: a February 2025 executive order directed the U.S. Treasury to develop a sovereign-wealth-fund plan, and Senator Sanders introduced an AI sovereign-wealth-fund bill in June 2026. **[tracker]**
- **TANF repurposing** is the most immediately actionable state-level mechanism, since states already control how block-grant dollars are spent: in 2023, states spent only about one-quarter of combined federal and state TANF funds on direct basic assistance, with 19 states spending less than 10%. Redirecting existing dollars requires no new federal legislation. **[tracker]**

The honest gap here: **beyond the Windfall Clause, none of these funding mechanisms currently has confirmed empirical or enacted-legislative traction** in this session's verification pass. They are design proposals worth presenting as options, not as evidence-backed policy with a track record.

## 5. Interaction with the existing safety net

Two structural issues determine whether guaranteed income actually helps displaced workers rather than just shuffling benefits around:

**The benefits cliff.** When guaranteed income payments trigger reductions in SNAP, Medicaid, housing assistance, or SSI, the net income gain for a recipient can be flat or negative. The Center for Guaranteed Income Research's policy framework proposes benefit disregards, waiver mechanisms, and legislative changes to allow simultaneous receipt of GI and existing supports — treating this as consequential as the transfer amount itself. **[tracker]**

**Complementarity, not substitution.** The same research center argues GI should be integrated into existing program architectures rather than treated as a replacement for wraparound services, workforce development, or social services, because financial insecurity itself creates cognitive and time burdens that block people from engaging with those other supports. **[tracker]**

**Unresolved question:** how guaranteed income or targeted cash would specifically interact with unemployment insurance and Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA), the two programs most directly aimed at displaced workers, was not addressed by any claim that survived this session's verification. This is a genuine gap in the confirmed evidence base and should be scoped as a follow-up research task before a primer makes specific UI/TAA-integration recommendations.

## 6. Legislative landscape (as of mid-2026)

**Federal:** H.R. 5830, the Guaranteed Income Pilot Program Act of 2025 (Rep. Bonnie Watson Coleman, introduced Oct 24, 2025), would establish a federally funded, HHS-administered pilot: 20,000 eligible taxpayers aged 18-65, split into 10,000 receiving monthly unconditional payments and 10,000 as an unpaid control group, over a 3-year randomized program. This bill is introduced and pending, not enacted. **[verified, high confidence]**

**State-level activity:** a widely circulated statistic claiming "20+ bills in 11 states" and "65+ bills in 15 states" propose GI or GI-adjacent cash policies did **not** survive verification this round (0-3 vote) and must not be repeated. **This is a real gap.** The confirmed evidence base for the state legislative landscape currently consists of exactly one federal bill. Anyone drafting the legislative-landscape section of a public-facing primer needs a dedicated, freshly sourced research pass on state activity before publishing a specific count or list of states.

## 7. Key debates and critiques

**Labor supply.** The strongest evidence-based critique is the ORUS finding of a roughly 4-percentage-point labor force participation decline and 1-2 fewer work hours per week among recipients (see Section 3). This cuts against the simpler "cash doesn't reduce work" talking point drawn from Stockton alone, and a credible primer should present both results rather than the more favorable one.

**Cost and scale.** Every pilot behind the positive employment and stabilization findings is small — 125 to 2,000 treatment participants, 2-3 years. None of this evidence has been tested at national, permanent scale, and the primer should say so explicitly wherever pilot results are cited as support for a specific national policy design.

**Inflation.** Pilot-scale guaranteed income (Stockton, Compton, ORUS) showed no measurable local inflation effect, but pilot scale is not national scale, and no verified evidence in this pass speaks to inflation risk under a nationwide program.

**Motivated framing.** Several primary sources behind the pro-cash-transfer evidence have a financial or institutional stake in the conclusion: OpenResearch is Sam Altman-backed, SEED was partly funded by the Economic Security Project, and JFI writes from an institutionally pro-GI position (disclosed above). This doesn't mean the underlying data is unsound — verification did not find factual problems with the core numbers — but a primer aimed at persuading skeptical audiences should name these interests rather than let a reader discover them independently.

**Work requirements politics.** The tracker's existing `guaranteed-income` policy entry notes that bipartisan opposition to guaranteed income concentrates specifically on the absence of work requirements, while bipartisan support exists for emergency, time-limited variants. **[tracker]** This suggests a targeted, time-bounded, displacement-triggered design is more politically viable than a permanent unconditional entitlement, at least as an opening position.

---

## 8. Recommendations

1. **Lead with targeted, not universal.** The displacement evidence (Section 2) and the political-feasibility evidence (Section 7) both point the same direction: a targeted guaranteed income for workers in verified AI-exposed occupations, phased toward broader eligibility only if displacement evidence broadens, is both more defensible and more fundable than an immediate universal program.
2. **Present the ORUS labor-supply finding alongside the Stockton employment-gain finding, always.** Selective citation is the single easiest way for this primer to lose credibility with a skeptical reader who checks the sources.
3. **Treat TANF repurposing as the fastest lever.** It requires no new federal legislation and the tracker's existing evidence base (`{{cite:212}}`) already establishes the scale of redirectable funds.
4. **Do not cite specific state-legislative-activity counts until a dedicated research pass confirms them.** The "20 bills in 11 states" statistic is currently unconfirmed and should not appear in any external document from this project until it is re-sourced.
5. **Pair any GI proposal with an explicit benefits-cliff fix.** Without disregards or waivers for SNAP, Medicaid, housing assistance, and SSI, a displaced worker's guaranteed income payment can produce zero or negative net gain.
6. **Scope a follow-up research task on UI/TAA interaction.** This is the clearest gap in the current evidence base for a primer that claims to address "transition support" specifically, since UI and TAA are the existing programs displaced workers actually touch first.
7. **Name the funders when citing pilot evidence.** Disclosing that OpenResearch is Altman-backed and SEED was ESP-funded costs little and preempts an obvious credibility attack.
