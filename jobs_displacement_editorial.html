Labor market · A feature in three verdicts
Is AI displacing workers yet?

The headline unemployment rate says one thing. Look closer and it says something more specific. Three questions, three verdicts.

A first pass; method and full series will live in a technical companion. Section 02 draws on the education / young-unemployment analysis.

01
Depth 01 · The aggregate
No.
The headline rate looks healthy, and it isn’t hiding withdrawal.

Okun’s law ties the change in unemployment to how fast the economy grows. If unemployment were rising faster than growth predicts, that would point to something structural, an AI-driven effect layered on the ordinary cycle. Each point below is a quarter; the line is the historical relationship, and the last four quarters are traced in sequence.

Okun’s law: GDP growth vs. change in unemployment

Low unemployment can mislead if people stop looking for work. The companion check is prime-age (25–54) participation, which strips out retirement. It sits above its pre-pandemic level, so the headline isn’t flattering.

Hiring has stalled where AI would bite first.

Displacement should show up as hiring drying up in specific industries. For each major sector we compare employment growth over the past year to its own average pace in 2015–2019. A large negative bar means that sector is adding jobs far more slowly than it used to, or shedding them outright.
