Purpose

This tracker curates and synthesizes research at the intersection of artificial intelligence, automation, and the economy. It aggregates peer-reviewed studies, policy reports, and investigative journalism into a structured database, then synthesizes that research into cross-cutting themes and a policy framework, to support institutional positioning, workforce strategy, and policy analysis.

What’s included

The database covers AI’s economic and labor impacts across 14 research categories, from labor markets and worker power to equity, criminal justice, and macroeconomic risk. Entries are selected for relevance to those impacts, the credibility of the source, and coverage across categories and viewpoints. Source types include peer-reviewed research, institutional and government reports, and investigative journalism, and every entry links to its original, publicly available publication.

How to read it

Three tabs. Research is the full, filterable database of individual sources. Themes synthesizes the database into cross-cutting questions, each with the evidence, what is working, the risks, and the implications. Policy Map groups policy interventions into four policy areas, listed alphabetically within each.

Confidence. Each theme is tagged Strong consensus (independent research streams converge), Emerging evidence (the direction is clear but still developing), or Contested (credible researchers reach different conclusions).

Lens. The General lens is written for a broad audience. The Focused lens filters to peer-reviewed and institutional research, organized around economic outcome domains.

Updates

The database is updated as new research emerges. The current build date appears in the footer, and a dated feed of additions and policy reviews is available in the What’s New log, reachable from the header (where a count marks entries added since your last look) and the footer.

Sourcing & AI

Every entry links to a named, publicly available source, and its summary is checked against that original. Summaries, key findings, and takeaways are drafted with AI assistance and reviewed for accuracy. They are orientation aids, not substitutes for the source: always consult the primary publication before citing or acting on a finding.

Is AI displacing workers yet? Follow the labor market down three levels.

The aggregate unemployment rate is a blunt instrument. To see displacement you have to ask three sharper questions: is unemployment higher than growth would predict, is the weakness spread evenly, and where has hiring actually decelerated. This is a first pass; each section links to a fuller technical treatment later.

01 · The aggregate

Is unemployment higher than we would expect?

Okun’s law ties the change in unemployment to how fast the economy grows: strong GDP growth pulls unemployment down, weak growth pushes it up. If unemployment were rising faster than growth alone predicts, that would be a sign of something structural, an AI-driven displacement effect layered on top of the ordinary business cycle. Below, each point is a quarter: real GDP growth on the horizontal axis, the change in the unemployment rate on the vertical. The line is the historical Okun relationship.

Okun’s law: GDP growth vs. change in unemployment

Unemployment can also mislead if people stop looking for work: they leave the labor force and the rate falls even as the job market weakens. So the companion check is prime-age (25–54) participation, which strips out retirement. If it were falling, low unemployment would be hiding withdrawal.

02 · Who

Is the weakness spread evenly, or concentrated?

If the aggregate looks fine, the next question is whether that average hides a group taking the hit. This chart takes each age and education group, asks what its unemployment rate should be given the overall rate (using its own pre-2020 relationship), and plots how far above or below that prediction it actually sits. Zero means “normal.” A positive value means that group has more unemployment than history says it should.

03 · Where

Where has job growth slowed the most?

Displacement should show up as hiring drying up in specific industries. For each major sector, we compare employment growth over the past year to its own average pace in the pre-pandemic years (2015–2019). A large negative bar means that sector is adding jobs far more slowly than it used to, or shedding them outright.

About

Purpose

This tracker is a curated research intelligence tool at the intersection of artificial intelligence, automation, and labor markets. It aggregates peer-reviewed studies, policy reports, and investigative journalism into a structured database — and synthesizes that research into strategic themes and policy frameworks — to support institutional positioning, workforce strategy, and policy analysis.

Created by

Christelle Prophete. This site is independently maintained and updated as new research emerges. The content does not reflect the opinions or perspectives of the creator.

Research tab

A filterable card database of individual research entries, tagged by category and geographic scope. Cards can be filtered by category and geography, or searched by title, source, or finding.

Themes tab

Cross-cutting syntheses of the research database. Each theme frames a key strategic question, presents an evidence consensus, identifies implications for institutional actors, and notes gaps in the current evidence base. Themes carry an editorial confidence rating: Strong consensus, Emerging evidence, or Contested.

Policy Map tab

Twenty-seven policy interventions organized by category and mapped to five stages of AI-driven labor disruption — from Repair (fixes needed regardless of AI's trajectory) through Transform (responses to large-scale displacement of the workforce). Each policy card includes a plain-language description, an account of the implementation challenge, and links to supporting research in the database. Does not constitute policy advice.
