Labor market · A descent in three depths

Is AI displacing workers yet?

The headline unemployment rate is a blunt instrument. To see displacement you have to descend: from the aggregate, to the distribution across workers, to the sectors doing the hiring. The answer changes as you go down. Each depth links to a fuller technical treatment to come.

Depth 01 · The aggregate

Is unemployment higher than growth alone would predict?

Okun’s law ties the change in unemployment to how fast the economy grows: strong GDP growth pulls unemployment down, weak growth pushes it up. If unemployment were rising faster than growth predicts, that would point to something structural, an AI-driven displacement effect layered on the ordinary business cycle. Each point is a quarter; the line is the historical Okun relationship. The last four quarters are traced in sequence.

Okun’s law: GDP growth vs. change in unemployment

Unemployment can also mislead if people stop looking for work: they leave the labor force and the rate falls even as the market weakens. The companion check is prime-age (25–54) participation, which strips out retirement. If it were falling, low unemployment would be hiding withdrawal.

Depth 02 · Across workers

Is the weakness spread evenly, or concentrated?

A healthy average can hide a group taking the hit. This takes each age-and-education group, asks what its unemployment rate should be given the overall rate (using its own pre-2020 relationship), and plots how far above or below that prediction it actually sits. Zero is normal. Positive means more unemployment than history says the group should have.
